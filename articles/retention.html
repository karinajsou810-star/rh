High-performance teams are not built by hiring well. They are built by keeping the right people long enough to matter.

Most founders think they have a hiring problem. They don't. They have a retention problem that surfaces at the hiring stage — because the people who should still be there aren't. Here is what actually drives talent out, what the data from the Portuguese market shows, and what builds a culture strong enough to keep them.

What retention actually means

Retention is the outcome of everything else a company does with its people.

Retention describes an organization's ability to keep its talent over time. But it is not a standalone metric — it is the result of how a company hires, onboards, develops, recognizes, and leads its people every day. When retention is low, the problem is almost never compensation alone. It is almost always a culture problem, a systems problem, or both.

For a founder building a team of 10 to 50 people, retention is not a nice-to-have. It is operational. Every person who leaves takes institutional knowledge, client relationships, and team momentum with them. In a team this size, there is no redundancy to absorb that loss. The remaining team feels it immediately — in workload, in morale, and in the questions they start asking themselves about the business.

High retention is not the goal. Building a high-performance team is the goal. Retention is what makes it possible. You cannot build a culture of consistent, high-level output with a revolving door.

The real cost of losing someone

The visible cost is the job posting. The invisible cost runs much deeper.

When someone leaves, most founders calculate the cost as time spent recruiting and the new hire's salary during ramp-up. That calculation misses most of the picture.

30–200%

of a departing employee's annual salary is the estimated cost of replacing them — once you account for lost productivity, recruitment, onboarding time, and the impact on the remaining team's engagement and output.

Source: Bussin & Mouton (2019); Sánchez, Martín & Iglésias (2019)

Beyond the financial impact, there is a cost that does not appear in any spreadsheet: the institutional knowledge that walks out the door. The client context that disappears. The team dynamic that shifts when a trusted colleague leaves. And the signal it sends to everyone who stays — because people notice when good people leave, and they start asking themselves why.

Research also shows that a strong employer brand directly reduces these costs. Companies with a well-defined employer brand receive more applications from qualified candidates, reducing time-to-hire and recruitment spend — while retaining the people already there at a higher rate (Bussin & Mouton, 2019).

Why your best people leave

They rarely leave for money. They leave because something was missing long before they handed in their notice.

Exit interviews are almost always too late and too polite to surface the real reasons. People default to “a better opportunity” or “career progression” because those answers are socially acceptable. The actual drivers are more specific — and more preventable.

Randstad's Country Report Portugal (2019) asked Portuguese workers directly what leads them to stay and what pushes them to leave. The results are telling.

What this data tells us is that compensation matters — but it is the floor, not the ceiling. The factors that retain people sit above it: security, environment, recognition, growth. When those are absent, a competitive salary is rarely enough to keep someone who has options.

A McKinsey study (2004) found

Factors such as a stimulating work environment, learning opportunities, and a sense of belonging to a team of like-minded people were rated more important by workers than a high salary. A good compensation is expected. What makes people stay — or leave — is everything around it.

What actually attracts talent

Attractiveness as an employer is not built during a hiring process. It is built every day before one starts.

In 2005, Berthon, Ewing and Hah developed the Employer Attractiveness Scale (EmpAt) — a framework of five dimensions that shape whether candidates choose to join an organization, and whether employees choose to stay. Understanding these dimensions is the foundation of any serious retention strategy.

Interest value

People are attracted to environments that are intellectually challenging, innovative, and that stimulate their creativity. Routine work without growth signals becomes a retention risk.

Social value

The quality of interpersonal relationships, team cohesion, and the overall atmosphere at work. In a study with business students (Eger et al., 2019), this was ranked the most important dimension of employer attractiveness — above compensation.

Economic value

Competitive salary, benefits, job security, and career advancement opportunities. This is the expected minimum — necessary but not sufficient to drive retention on its own.

Development value

Recognition, professional growth, and the sense that skills and contributions are valued. When this is missing, high performers — the ones with options — are the first to recalibrate.

Application value

The opportunity to apply knowledge meaningfully and contribute to something beyond the individual role. People stay longer when their work feels purposeful and connected to a larger outcome.

Research applying the EmpAt scale consistently shows that social and development dimensions outperform economic ones as predictors of long-term retention. Companies that invest in team culture, recognition, and growth pathways retain talent more effectively than those competing on salary alone.

Employer branding is not marketing

The biggest mistake companies make is confusing communication about the company with the experience inside it.

Employer branding is not what you say you are. It is what people feel when they work for you — and what they say when they leave.

I see the same pattern repeatedly. Companies invest in the visible layer: office photos, a careers page that says “we are a family,” an institutional video with motivational music and smiling employees, an award won through an internal survey. None of these things are inherently wrong. The problem is treating them as the cause of a good culture rather than a symptom of one.

What companies do thinking it is employer branding

Posting office photos with plants and colourful furniture. Writing “we are a family” on the careers page. Producing a motivational video with employees smiling. Winning a “best place to work” award based on an internal form. Listing benefits on paper that nobody actually uses.

The problem is not that these things are bad. It is that they are symptoms of a good culture — not the cause of one.

A company that runs structured feedback conversations, promotes consistently, explains difficult decisions instead of hiding them, and has onboarding that actually works does not need to spend €50k on an employer branding video. People talk about it without being asked.

What happens when you invest only in the outer layer: candidates arrive with expectations the day-to-day cannot confirm. Turnover increases. Glassdoor reviews contradict what is on LinkedIn. And the company gets stuck in a cycle of attracting to lose.

Employer branding starts on the first day of work — not in the first job posting. The internal reality has to match the external promise. When it does not, no amount of communication fixes it. Research on leading Portuguese organizations confirms this: understanding and delivering on what employees actually experience is as important as — or more important than — communicating value propositions to external candidates (Sousa, Ferreira, Arriscado & Quesado, 2016).

Where to start

The founders I work with are not indifferent to their people. They are overwhelmed — and retention keeps getting pushed to next quarter.

This is the pattern I see most consistently: a founder who genuinely cares about the team, is working at full capacity, and has no bandwidth left to think strategically about people. Retention is not ignored — it is perpetually deferred. Until someone resigns. And then it becomes urgent.

By that point, the cost is already paid. The question is whether the next departure was preventable — and whether anything has been built to reduce the likelihood of it happening again.

The first thing I look for when I work with a team is not a problem. It is a gap between what the founder knows and what the team has access to. In almost every engagement, the standards exist. The expectations exist. The sense of what good looks like exists. It has never been made explicit, which means it cannot be transferred, taught, or replicated — not to the next hire, nor the one after that.

That gap is where retention breaks down. Not in the benefits package. Not in the salary band. In the distance between what the founder intended and what the team actually experiences day to day.

What this looks like in practice

At Mercearia Elite, a growing hospitality business, new hires were taking up to two weeks to reach full operating standard — not because they lacked capability, but because there was no structured way to transfer the institutional knowledge that existed with the owner. Customer wait times averaged 30 minutes. The team communicated actively. But none of that translated into a consistent, repeatable experience for someone joining the business.

Once the onboarding system was in place and service standards were documented and taught, new hires reached full readiness in three days. Wait times dropped to under five minutes. The team did not change. The infrastructure around them did.

That is where I start with every engagement: not with a framework, but with a diagnosis. What does the team actually experience versus what the founder intends? Where is knowledge informal when it needs to be structural? What is the first thing that breaks when a new person joins?

The answers to those questions tell you exactly where to build first — and what, once in place, makes everything else more stable. You do not need a People team to start. You need the right questions asked of the right people, and someone who knows what to do with the answers.
