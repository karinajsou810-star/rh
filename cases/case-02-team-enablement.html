Case 02 · Team Enablement

Fixing the gaps behind slow service

When communication breaks inside a team, the customer is the first to feel it.

Focus
Internal communication & enablement
Sector
Hospitality
My role
Diagnosis & intervention design
Method
Mixed‑method diagnosis + training

The context

A hospitality team was losing ground where it hurts most: slow service, an inconsistent experience, and customer reviews that flagged both. The owner assumed it was an effort problem. It wasn't. The team cared, the information didn't flow.

The real problem

Most "service problems" are communication problems wearing a disguise.

Between shifts, between roles, between the floor and the kitchen, handoffs were verbal, informal, and easy to drop. Nobody was failing on purpose. The system around them just had no structure to lean on, so under pressure, things slipped and customers waited.

The approach

Diagnose first. Build second. No guessing.
01
Diagnosed from three angles
An owner interview, a staff survey, and an analysis of public customer reviews, so the picture wasn't one person's opinion.
02
Mapped where communication broke
Pinpointed the handoffs and moments where information was being lost, and how that translated into wait times.
03
Built a modular training program
Structured modules on customer service, order flow, and effective communication, designed to be repeatable for every new hire.
04
Created the supports to sustain it
A communication overhaul and assets so the new standard stuck after the project ended.

What was delivered

3
data sources triangulated in the diagnosis
4
training modules built for the team
5
communication assets created to sustain it
0→1
from ad‑hoc to a structured enablement system

The result was a repeatable enablement system, not a one‑off pep talk, built to cut wait times and lift both the customer and the employee experience. A new hire could now be brought up to the same standard instead of learning by osmosis.

The takeaway

Fix the flow of information, and the experience fixes itself.

This is the work I bring to any growing team: turning informal, in‑someone's‑head processes into systems a team can actually run, so quality doesn't depend on who happens to be on shift.
