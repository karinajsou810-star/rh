Case 03 · Engagement & Retention

What actually keeps people engaged

Retention isn't about pay. The data points somewhere most companies overlook.

Focus
Engagement, motivation & retention
Sector
Food & beverage
My role
Study design & statistical analysis
Method
Quantitative survey + SPSS

The context

A 40‑person company wanted an honest answer to a question most businesses only guess at: what really drives whether people stay motivated, satisfied, and stay at all? Instead of assuming, we ran the numbers.

The real question

Everyone says engagement matters. Almost nobody measures what actually moves it.

The hypothesis: that internal communication, not perks, not pay alone, is what drives motivation, satisfaction, and ultimately retention. The job was to test it with real data, not opinion.

The approach

A proper quantitative study, designed, run, and analysed end to end.
01
Framed the study around one question
How does internal communication influence motivation and satisfaction at work?
02
Designed and ran the survey
Structured across three dimensions, communication, motivation, and satisfaction, on a measured scale.
03
Collected and cleaned the data
Gathered responses across the company and prepared the dataset for analysis.
04
Ran the statistical analysis
Univariate and bivariate analysis in SPSS to find the real relationships in the data.

What the data showed

40
person company studied
21
survey responses analysed
4.4/5
average job‑satisfaction score
SPSS
univariate + bivariate analysis

The finding was clear: where internal communication was strong, motivation and satisfaction followed, and turnover risk dropped with them. Satisfaction tracked with how informed and heard people felt, and with their tenure. Weak communication, the data implied, would have pulled all three the other way.

The takeaway

People stay where they're communicated with and feel they belong. That's the foundation everything else is built on.

It's why I treat retention as a communication and systems problem first, and why engagement surveys, run too late, miss the point. The signal is there long before the resignation; you just have to be set up to see it.
