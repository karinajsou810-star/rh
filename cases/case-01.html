The challenge

Hiring had quietly become overwhelming.

Job descriptions arrived as a few vague lines. Screening, scheduling, interview notes, and candidate follow-ups all ran on top of an already full day to day, and each step changed shape depending on who picked it up. When the team needed to make a decision, they depended on scattered notes, subjective impressions, and whoever remembered what.

Candidates felt it first. After each interview, they waited for feedback without knowing how long. Follow-ups depended on someone remembering to do them. And with 40 open roles simultaneously, qualified people were quietly falling between phases, which is the most expensive way to lose them.

With a market average time-to-hire of 23 to 45 days, the team knew their process could be faster. But no one owned the candidate journey from the first call to the first day, so every improvement was impossible to measure, or sustain.

"There were processes that depended on whoever had conducted the interview being available to explain what they had seen. The decision about a candidate was stuck in a person, not in a system."
Ana Barreiro · HR, Public Employment and Training Institute
The solution

A structured hiring process, implemented end to end.

Every process now starts with a proper intake conversation: what this person needs to do well in six months, not which keywords match the posting. That profile becomes the screening criteria, and the criteria become a structured interview guide, documented and implemented, so every candidate is measured against the same bar.

Around that human approach, supporting systems keep the day to day moving. Each remote interview turns into a structured, shared record the moment the session ends, so decisions stop depending on memory and start relying on consistent information.

And the candidate experience is cared for at every stage. When a candidate advances, they receive confirmation and next steps immediately. When they don't, they receive honest, respectful feedback. No candidate is left without a response, so the institute's name gets stronger even when the answer is no.

"From the moment the system went live, the team started arriving at each decision with structured information instead of impressions. And candidates started receiving an immediate response, not when someone remembered."
Ana Barreiro · HR, Public Employment and Training Institute
